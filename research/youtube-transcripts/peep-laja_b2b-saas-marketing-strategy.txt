Speaking of messaging, you’re known for advocating conversational language and using the customer’s own words in your marketing. How do marketers get better at that, and how does it impact the buyer journey? What metrics or dials should they watch when testing messages for resonance?

Messaging analysis has four heuristics: clarity, value, relevance, and differentiation. Clarity is the most important—can the reader understand what you’re saying? Use conversational language and avoid jargon. Value is the promise of what’s delivered: is it specific, and do people want it? If you only say “grow your business,” that’s unremarkable. Relevance means your message speaks to the buyer’s priorities and challenges. If you say “make more money with email marketing,” that’s no longer compelling. Differentiation is crucial in SaaS, where most companies claim to do similar things like “send beautiful newsletters.” Don’t rely on generic claims; explain what makes you different.

On executive buy-in, brand vs. performance, and long-term strategy A recurring issue is the messaging that originates from the founder or a high-level executive, sometimes called the hippo (highest-paid person’s opinion). How do you shift that, and how do you convince executives to adopt different messaging and positioning when market conditions have changed?

Messaging needs to be tested with buyers, and you should show that it’s still current and relevant. If you’re not Shopify and you want to compete with Shopify, you have to say something different. Use message testing to determine whether your positioning resonates and differentiates you.

Branding as a differentiator is also important for CAC. Beyond direct response advertising, you need brand awareness and saliency. When you’re trying to be considered in a crowded space, brand plays a large role. The research suggests that companies investing in brand awareness and distinctiveness tend to see stronger activation results over time.

If CAC is rising, what can marketers do? Education is key. You need credibility—trust from leadership. Look at data from sources like the IPA (in the UK) and the long-term revenue impact of continuing brand advertising during downturns. When executives see data showing that brand investment pays off in the long term, they’re more likely to commit.

On time constraints, prioritization, and doing less but better When marketers are overwhelmed with time and budget constraints, what should they do? Start by trimming away low-impact activities. If you pause them for a short period and nothing happens, you’re likely wasting time. If you pause for longer, you risk missing important opportunities. The idea is to do fewer things, but do them better. Look for the activities where you’re not just doing what everyone else is doing; pivot to approaches that break from the norm.
