Part One: Mindset

This is to help you and your marketing team, your leadership team, get into the mindset of content creators. The purpose of this entire framework is to help companies build sustainable media engines through in-house creators. The first part of this framework is Mindset, which sets out to provide an answer for the question: What is good content? You hear it all the time, right? Just make good content. But what does that even mean?

So, to figure it out, I asked myself: What is one content asset or one type of content that we would all agree on that is good content? So I thought: Super Bowl commercials—everyone loves them. But why, right? I gave that some thought and found three attributes: they usually show you something new or reveal something, so they’re insightful; they’re usually funny or have some emotional appeal, so they’re entertaining; and they’re usually when they do something different or build these commercials on really creative or unique concepts, so they’re interesting. In other words: insightful, entertaining, and interesting.

In the B2B SaaS context, you could have a great blog post or a great LinkedIn post, a video of you talking about something—so long as you’re providing insightful substance, that could be content. On the other end of the spectrum, you have content with very high production costs, like corporate-style reenactments or ads, but the key is how you present it.

To sort content, we break it into three groups: Type 1, Type 2, and Type 3. Type 1 content is your insightful substance (blogs, LinkedIn posts, podcasts). Type 2 content is insightful substance presented in an interesting format (animated explainers, infographics like the Periodic Table of SEO). Type 3 is insightful substance presented in an interesting format and built on an entertaining concept (commercials, funny skits, marketing songs). The more you see content this way, the easier it is to identify what makes high-performing assets.

How to Use Type Classifications

Would this be a way to structure your workflow? Begin with insightful substance, then look for ways to present it in a more interesting format, and then raise the bar with entertaining concepts? And would this translate to budgeting or asset complexity? Yes, that’s one way to use it. For example, you could have a straightforward LinkedIn post as Type 1, create a live-stream video while you play a video game and discuss the topic as Type 2, and even write rap songs about the topic as Type 3. You can create multiple, very different content assets from one topic rather than just repurposing a single blog post into various formats.

Important caveat: This isn’t a progression system where you must move from Type 1 to Type 2 to Type 3. It depends on your goals, objectives, timeline, and resources. Some topics may require Type 1 only; others may benefit from Type 2 or Type 3.

Production Value and Brand Fit

What about the tension between casual content and a carefully crafted brand? And can low-budget pieces still align with a strong brand?

It’s a both/and situation. You shouldn’t start with entertaining content before you have strategic, narrative, and identity foundations. The latter is Part Two: Purpose. In Part Two, we cover a progression system: Top-Down, Middle-Out, and Bottom-Up content. This helps you map content to the audience at different levels of an organization, from leaders to implementers to end users, ensuring you align your content with your narrative while respecting brand guidelines and resource constraints.

Purpose

Part Two is Purpose. Before you get into tactics, you must understand the purpose behind all the content you’ll be creating. We have three purposes:

Top-Down Content: Content aimed at reaching leaders and decision-makers who can drive company-wide change. Formats: podcasts, webinars, etc.—narrative-driven content.
Middle-Out Content: Content aimed at the people within the target organization who will tactically implement change (e.g., SDR managers, heads of customer success).
Bottom-Up Content: Content aimed at end users within the target organization who will drive awareness and admiration from the ground up (e.g., entertaining or relatable content that supports product-led growth).
This framework helps you define and segment your audience more precisely than traditional buyer personas, and it helps marketing support the sales team with content tailored to the actual day-to-day challenges of different roles within target organizations.

Audiences and Audience Segmentation

Top-Down: Leaders and decision-makers who can drive change. Middle-Out: People who will implement changes within the organization. Bottom-Up: End users who will drive awareness and adoption.

This approach helps you map who you should be talking to and what content will reach them, and it helps you build a more precise audience profile for your campaigns.

Audience and Objectives Alignment

Your audience segmentation should reflect both the people you are connected with and the people you are trying to reach as a company. For example, if your end users are SAS account executives, your LinkedIn feed should reflect that audience, not just other marketers. Your objectives should reflect the goals across the organization—marketing, sales, customer success, product, etc.—and content should align with those goals from the outset.

Five Prerequisites of Ideation

Part Three: Strategy includes two sections: The Five Prerequisites of Ideation and The Realm of Relevancy.

The Five Prerequisites of Ideation (deep understanding required of five areas)

Your Product: What is it? What does it do? Who does it help? How does it help? Do you know all the features? Can you show someone how to use it?
Your Narrative: What is it? Why is it needed? How do we adopt it? How do we adapt to it? Do you have examples? Do you have research to back it up?
Your Audience: Who are they? What are their challenges and goals? What platforms do they use? What are their actual interests? What segments make up your audience?
Your Objectives: What are your business objectives, marketing objectives, sales objectives, customer success objectives, product objectives? You should know everyone’s objectives.
Five Different Content Formats: Can your team create a wide range of different content assets (animated explainer videos, podcast episodes, infographics, commercials, blog posts, etc.)? Or are you stuck with a narrow format (ebook, blog post, newsletter)?
Realm of Relevancy

This is a bridge that helps map your strategic narrative to everyday content topics. It helps embed the strategic narrative into your day-to-day content so that it’s not just a one-off piece but a living, consistent narrative across formats and channels.

How to Use Ideation Prerequisites

If you don’t have a deep understanding of your product, narrative, audience, objectives, or formats, you’ll get stuck with ideation. If you do have those, you can start ideating content aligned with the narrative and the audience.
