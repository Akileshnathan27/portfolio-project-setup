Part 1: Choosing a Sub-Niche

This is critical. I see people on LinkedIn wildly unfocused. For example, one person’s week of content might be: Monday, how to be a great sales leader; Tuesday, a funny thing my kid did; Wednesday, Happy Father’s Day; Thursday, my favorite Bob Marley song; Friday, another sales leadership tip. This is what I see when one person writes about a bunch of different things.
You have to write about the same thing or record video about the same thing every single day. People need to know what you talk about and what they’ll learn from you.
It is a cluttered, noisy, busy world, and LinkedIn is the same. So you have to stand out; people should know exactly who you serve and exactly how you help them. Each morning, when I write content, my audience of 115,000 knows they’re going to get tips on how to grow on LinkedIn.
The second biggest problem I see: people have no niche. For example, “I’ll help you grow if you’re a company.” “Best marketing strategies for companies.” “How to get leads for your company.” “Make sales for your company.” “Company websites.” These are six wildly different types of businesses. You can’t just talk about all companies; you can’t just talk about people. You have to pick a niche: who exactly do you help, and what exactly do you help them do?
Remember: when you talk to everybody, you talk to nobody. And so, for your morning content, you want a sub-niche. In fact, you want followers to be able to self-identify as prospects and learn from you every day.
If someone asks, “Who’s the best guy on LinkedIn to learn about LinkedIn growth?” I hope people say it’s me. If someone asks, “Who’s the best guy to learn about baking chocolate chip cookies?” I hope nobody says me, because I don’t talk about those things.
Stay laser-focused. You can gain a tribe if you’re talking about one thing. Here’s the broad topic → niche → sub-niche formula. For example, “athletes” is too broad; “runners” is a niche; “marathon runners” is a sub-niche. From there, you can discuss shoes, training for long distance, outfits, etc.
There is a fine line: be specific but not so small that you can’t grow. If you can get 100 to 1,000 true fans, you can build a business.
Six ways to sub-niche down: price, demographics, psychographics, vertical and industry, geographics, and level of quality. (Note: The speaker will share the slide with you, and there will be a replay.)

Part 2: Creating Meaningful Content

If you want to grow your audience of prospective buyers, you must create meaningful content.
Two major problems people face: they focus on the wrong outcome, and they chase dopamine (likes, comments) instead of dollars (customers).
Example: a tear-jerking UPS driver story got thousands of likes but didn’t move the needle for a business. Polls and gimmicks create engagement, but they don’t move people toward becoming customers.
The goal of every piece of content should be to move people into your tribe; tribe members become buyers.
Three proven ways to get people into your tribe:
Polarize: Have a strong opinion. Some will hate it, but the majority who share your belief will join your tribe.
Teach: Provide content with clear, actionable steps. For example, an eight-step process for landing a client on LinkedIn, or a four-step process for raising your rates as a consultant.
Entertain: Be entertaining; even then, you’ll likely polarize a portion of your audience, which is okay.
The Content Matrix: A quick tool to generate content ideas. Left side lists the topics; top lists eight structures for posts: actionable, motivational, analytical, contrarian, observation, X vs. Y, present for future, listicle, plus two more: your upcoming week and your highs and lows from the week. This helps you create six to eight ideas quickly.
Writing Process: A three-step process called Trailer, Meet, and Recap. The speaker writes consistently and shares his process so others can replicate it.
Affliction: Knowledge Blindness

Everyone has something valuable to share. Two questions to ask yourself:
Who deserves to teach something?
At what point does someone become an expert?
Most thought leaders you follow simply have more experience. Don’t compare yourself to others; start where you are. Create content for the audience you were two to three years ago. Birth and death of knowledge: you teach those behind you while you’re a little ahead of those you teach.
Part 3: Building a Relevant Tribe

To build a tribe, you must give people a reason to follow you. There are three proven paths:
Be a Leader: Share experiences you’ve accumulated. Ask yourself where you were two to three years ago, who is in that situation now, and whether you can help them reach your level faster and with fewer mistakes.
Discover and Curate on Behalf of Your Audience: If you don’t have the direct expertise, you can discover and curate the best insights on behalf of your audience. An example is Sumit Garg, who breaks down shareholder letters and other materials for his audience.
Interview and Distribute: Interview experts and share the insights with your audience. Chris Flatzuk on Twitter and Adam from The Pause Cast are good examples.
You can mix and match these approaches to grow your audience in your sub-niche.
Part 4: Generating Leads

There are two proven ways to generate leads: inbound leads and hunting outbound leads.
Inbound Leads: A three-step process:
Write a daily post each morning to draw readers to your profile.
Use Featured sections on your profile: Top of Funnel (TOF) and Bottom of Funnel (BOF).
BOF: A direct offer (e.g., your course) for customers ready to buy.
TOF: A free resource (e.g., Free LinkedIn Advice) to collect emails via an evergreen article.
Automated emails: When people opt in via the evergreen guide, they subscribe to your weekly newsletter, and you can consistently drive interest and sales.
The evergreen article is a robust, updated guide that remains valuable over years and collects email addresses.
Outbound Leads: A simple two-step process:
Set up Google Alerts for your ideal prospects (e.g., healthcare companies raising a Series A).
Reach out on Fridays with a simple, congratulatory message, avoiding hard selling. If they respond, you can move the conversation toward offering your services. This method has yielded customers without pushy sales pitches.
This approach emphasizes automation, consistency, and genuine engagement.
